India’s delicate balance on the Gaza Crisis

  • P R Kumaraswamy

The eleven-day conflict between Israel and Hamas in the Gaza Strip in May is a wake-up call for the international community of the urgency of the Palestinian question and the need to work towards resolving the prolonged problem. The recent violence was the fourth major conflict since Israel unilaterally withdrew from the Gaza Strip in September 2005.

Like the earlier one, this also witnessed a barrage of rocket attacks by Palestinian militant groups and the Israeli retaliatory strikes, causing enormous losses on the Palestinian side and severe destruction to properties and infrastructure in the Gaza Strip. Though the Palestinian militant groups fired about 4,000 rockets, their technological limitations and effective Israeli counter-measures contained the casualties on the Israeli side; while at least 220 Palestinians, including several militants, were killed, and a dozen were killed in Israel. Unlike the past, the Israel-Hamas conflict was not confined to the Gaza Strip, and for the first time, it also spread to pockets of the West Bank. Though the damage was not significant, they conveyed restlessness among the larger Palestinian population vis-à-vis Israel. Due to larger strategic calculations, especially over the nuclear negotiations in Vienna, Iran restrained Hizballah— Lebanese Shia militant group—from joining the conflict. For the same reason, Tehran’s support of Hamas during the conflict was symbolic, not substantial.

In an ominous development, the crisis also spread to Israel proper and witnessed Arab-Jewish violence in several mixed cities and towns. For the first time since 1948, Israel saw communal riots between the two communities including lynching, desecration of gravestones and even the torching of a synagogue. This will undermine the ability of Arab parties to break the psychological barrier and, for the first time, join a government since the formation of Israel.

With able assistance from Egyptian leader Fattah al-Sisi, President Joe Biden was able to bring about a ceasefire, and this was immediately followed by the visit of Secretary of State Tony Blinken to the Middle East to shore up the gains. While Israel and Hamas claimed victory, it is essential to restart the Israeli-Palestinian negotiations stalled for years. Indeed, no meaningful negotiations happened ever since Benjamin Netanyahu became the Israeli Prime Minister in March 2009.

India, which is currently a non-permanent member of the UN Security Council, reacted to the crisis in a sober but delicate fashion. The killing of Indian caregiver Soumya Santosh in Ashkelon, about 21 kilometres from Gaza, added a personal dimension to the crisis. Disapproving violence New Delhi categorically condemned the ‘indiscriminate rocket firing from Gaza’ while merely noting the ‘immense suffering and (resultant) deaths’ due to Israel’s ‘retaliatory strikes into Gaza.’ This did not go down well with traditional friends of Palestine within the country but is in line with a more balanced stand India has been adopting since the normalization of relations with Israel in January 1992.

Unlike the past, India no longer condemns Israel for all the problems and violence in the region and is mindful of the shifts vis-à-vis the Palestinian question. In recent years, several key Arab countries have moved closer to the Jewish State despite the prolongation of the Palestinian stateless. Countries like the UAE, Bahrain, Morocco and Sudan have prioritized bilateral relations over their commitments to the Palestinian cause and normalized relations with Israel.

At the same time, for India, maintaining the status quo on the ground, especially in East Jerusalem, is essential if tension is to be avoided in the future. In its opinion, recent measures by Israel in the Arab neighbourhood of the old city, especially during the holy month of Ramadan, ignited the current conflict. Hence, it cautioned Israel from taking against any moves that could cause tension.

Interestingly, India recognized the importance of the city of Jerusalem to both Muslims and Jews, and in its statements before the Security Council, it referred to Haram Al-Sharif/ Temple Mount. The usage of both expressions tacitly but firmly rejects the traditional narrative of exclusive Islamic ownership of the area.

Even while urging for an ‘immediate de-escalation’, India did not miss out on the big picture. The Israel occupation of the Arab-Palestinian lands is the central issue. No enduring and lasting peace in the region is possible without the realization of Palestinian Statehood. Hence, like much of the international community, India reiterated its support for the two-state solution, namely, Israel and the State of Palestine coexisting side by side. This support came with an interesting caveat. Since its statement during the visit of Palestinian President Mahmoud Abbas to New Delhi in May 2017, India does not refer to East Jerusalem as the capital of the future Palestinian State. This was maintained during its intervention in the UNSC on the Gaza crisis. Likewise, its statements do not refer to the June 1967 borders as a dividing line between Israel and Palestine. In its view, the questions of capital and borders are too sensitive to be decided by third parties but should be left to Israel and Palestine to reach an agreement. While supporting a Palestinian State is its principled position, India strongly feels that the cantors must be worked by the parties concerned through accommodation and negotiations.

Towards this end, India called for the restarting of the negotiations between Israel and the internationally recognized Palestine National Authority. At this time, New Delhi does not see any role for the militant Palestinian group Hamas that has been seeking to undermine the official Palestinian leadership since the late 1980s.

India seeks closer relations with both parties; if technology cooperation with Israel could contribute to its economic ascendance, continued support for the Palestinian cause could enhance its political influence in the Middle East. A conflictual relation between the two parties does not serve the Indian interests. Hence, frequent bouts of tension, conflict and violence cannot be remedied without addressing the larger issue of the Palestinian cause and their inalienable right to statehood. While urging the political route to the problem, India also does not visualize any mediatory role but is content in supporting various international, especially by the Quartet. efforts in this direction.
